Welcome to your grep training, Detective.

You have 30+ files in this folder. Inside one of them is a critical
witness statement. Your job: find it using the `grep` command.

--- WHAT IS GREP? ---

`grep` is short for "Global Regular Expression Print." That sounds
fancy, but all it really means is: SEARCH FOR A WORD IN FILES.

Instead of opening every file and reading it manually (which would
take forever with 30 files), you can search ALL of them at once.

--- HOW TO USE GREP ---

Basic usage:
  grep "search term" filename.txt

Search ALL .txt files at once:
  grep "search term" *.txt

Search ALL files in the folder:
  grep "search term" *

Show the filename too (great for many files):
  grep -l "search term" *

Case-insensitive search (find "WORD", "word", "Word", etc.):
  grep -i "search term" *

Show the line number where it was found:
  grep -n "search term" filename.txt

--- YOUR MISSION ---

A witness was spotted at the harbor. Her last name is a common
Spanish surname. She appears somewhere in the report files.

Use grep to find her. Try:
  grep -i "witness" report_*.txt

Or search for a Spanish-sounding last name:
  grep -i "santos" *

Or search more broadly:
  grep -i "harbor" *

When you find the witness's full name and the report she appears in,
you've completed the main challenge.

BONUS: How many times does the word "alert" appear across ALL log
files? Try:
  grep -i "alert" *.log | wc -l

The `wc -l` part counts the lines. Powerful combo!

--- REMEMBER ---

`grep` is one of the most important detective tools in the Terminal.
Professional cybersecurity analysts use grep hundreds of times per day
to search through logs, find security threats, and track down clues.

Now go find that witness!
